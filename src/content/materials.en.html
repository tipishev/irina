❗ What is not suitable

Do not buy:

  • dry children's watercolor (Swedish vattenfärg, "honey" and similar sets);
  • gouache and watercolor in tubes;
  • acrylic paints from Tiger stores.

Why?

Professional paints contain more pigment, so the colors turn out brighter, richer, mix better, and are used much more economically.

Children's watercolor is designed for very young kids and does not allow for a quality result. Tube paints require certain skills to work with, so we don't use them in our studio classes.

Which pencils do I need?

A set that contains different grades — H (hard) and B (soft): 2H, H, HB, 2B, 3B, 6B. You can also buy them individually at specialty stores. Best brands: Faber-Castell and Koh-i-Noor.

Good to know! Pencils should be stored in a hard case (metal or wood) so that they don't get dropped. When a pencil falls, the lead inside breaks, and then you can sharpen it endlessly.

What is the difference between natural and synthetic brushes?

Brushes come flat and round in cross-section, made of different materials. Goat and hog bristle are for oil; pony, squirrel, raccoon, and kolinsky are for watercolor. Synthetic is for more aggressive paints — gouache, acrylic, tempera, oil. The higher the number, the thicker and more expensive the brush. The most expensive are kolinsky and squirrel. We need all kinds! Do NOT buy goat or hog bristle!

We need different brushes. Painting a background with a nail brush is very uncomfortable and slow, and painting mom's eyelashes on a portrait with a flat brush is also awkward. So buying just one set and thinking "that's fine, the child has brushes" won't work. A little comparison for clarity: how many brushes does mom have in her makeup bag? A lot, and they're all different — fluffy and large for powder, thin and firm for eyeliner and brows, synthetic for lipstick and foundation. Why? Right — how do you draw a good eyeliner with a broad flat? Or how do you dip a fluffy brush into cream foundation?

It's exactly the same in painting! Of course there are virtuosos who paint their whole face with a single cotton swab, but let's not go to extremes :) So for watercolor we need natural-hair brushes — watercolor loves water. Natural bristles hold water well, synthetic ones don't. It is real animal hair — fragile and brittle, like our own hair, so you need to take care of them!

If a brush has been bent, splayed, or curled — if it was left in water too long — you need to soap it thickly into a lather and pull it back into its original shape, then leave it to dry that way. Before use, rinse it — the brush will regain its shape. Acrylic and gouache are thicker, denser, more aggressive paints — so the brush should be springy, and synthetic works better.

If a synthetic brush has become fluffy and lost its shape, boil it in water for 5 minutes and it will take its original shape back. If acrylic was not washed out, the brush becomes as hard as stone — only professional cleaners can help, but there's a chance, so don't throw brushes away! Store brushes in a case or holder, cherish them, and wash them with soap. Natural brushes can even be washed with conditioner.

What should the palette be like?

Plastic, large, with deep wells and a mixing area. Wooden and paper palettes are not suitable for watercolor — only for creamy paints. If you don't wash acrylic off the palette immediately, it's ruined — acrylic can't be cleaned off!

Which watercolor is good?

"Ours" — non-local ones exist too, but the price is like an airplane wing! Do NOT buy anything else — and do not buy tubes!

Why aren't honey watercolors, dry sets, or tubes suitable? Again — pigment saturation. These paints are not professional; for 3+ year olds their consumption is high but the result is poor. Honey watercolors are too soft — kids dig them down to the bottom of the pan, ruining paint and brushes, and end up with 100 shades of gray on paper and no clean color. Dry sets have the same problem, but they're hard and wear down natural brushes very quickly — plus they contain gouache elements, so there's no transparency. Tube watercolors — yes, there are professional ones, but the price is huge, and learning to work with that texture from scratch is difficult, while non-professional tubes are just a strange goo, not watercolor.

Good to know! Before working with watercolor, moisten it a little, and after finishing dry it well at home — otherwise it will smear and mix into a mess.

Which gouache is good?

"Ours" and Chinese. Do NOT buy anything else!

Why isn't other gouache suitable? As dissection shows, not all paints labeled "gouache" are good and right, so if you buy paints not on the list — it's at your own risk, 50/50. What does "bad" mean? Different chemical composition, poor coverage; they behave like rubber or slime, smear across the paper like snot, mix poorly, don't cover each other, and are dull and semi-transparent.

Good to know! Gouache dries out and gets moldy — it happens. For long-term storage, pour a little water on top (without going overboard) and close tightly. Before use, check it, stir with a stick, cut off any mold with a knife, and keep using it. If the gouache is already dry as a stone — it's dead. If it still looks rubbery — there's a chance to save it!

What is plein air and why is it needed?

Plein air (from French en plein air — "in the open air") is the creative method of painting pictures or studies directly from nature, under natural light. It's necessary to learn to convey the richness of colors, aerial perspective, and the fleeting states of nature (like the play of light and shadow), which cannot be accurately reproduced indoors.

Yes, it's important and mandatory for everyone! Why?

Conveying light and color: natural sunlight dramatically changes color perception, adding reflections from the sky, grass, and buildings.

Training visual awareness: the sun moves, so shadows and lighting change every 15–20 minutes. This develops reaction speed and the ability to capture the essence of what's happening.

Natural perspective: outdoors the artist learns to build aerial perspective correctly — to understand how distant objects become paler and lose contrast through layers of air.

Plein air is a field effort in the city or in nature — meaning we carry everything with us like turtles. So it's important to equip yourself with lighter and smaller materials. Optimizing the volume and weight of everything you carry is very important. This is where we need folding jars, stools, small palettes, compact folders, folding paint sets, A4, A5 and square sketchbooks, and clips so the paper doesn't fly away.
